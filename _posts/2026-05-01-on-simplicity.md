The urge to add more is almost always the wrong instinct.

More features. More words. More caveats and qualifications and edge cases handled "just in case." We reach for complexity as if it signals seriousness — as if a thing that is easy to understand must not have required much thought to create.

The opposite is usually true. Simplicity is harder. It demands that you understand something well enough to strip it down to what actually matters, and then have the discipline to stop there.

## The cost of complexity

Every piece of complexity you add to a system has to be paid for. Someone has to understand it, maintain it, explain it. The complexity doesn't disappear — it just gets distributed, spread across everyone who ever touches the thing.

In writing, this shows up as sentences that do too much. A sentence that contains two ideas usually does justice to neither. In software, it shows up as functions that handle seventeen edge cases and require a paragraph of comments to explain what they're doing.

> Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away.

Antoine de Saint-Exupéry said that about aircraft design, but it applies everywhere.

## Simplicity is not the same as easy

This is the part people miss. Simplicity isn't about dumbing things down or refusing to engage with hard problems. A simple solution to a hard problem is a great achievement. A simple solution to an easy problem is just... a solution.

The goal is to match the complexity of your solution to the complexity of the problem, and no more. Not less — a solution that pretends a problem is simpler than it is will break at the edges. But definitely no more.

When I find myself adding complexity to something — a codebase, an essay, a plan — I try to ask: is this complexity in service of the problem, or in service of my own sense of thoroughness? Often it's the latter. And the latter, you can cut.
